Transformer oils are stable at high temperatures and have excellent electrical insulating properties

A Primer on Transformer Oils


Updated: Nov 16, 2022


A transformer oil or insulating oil is an oil that is stable at high temperatures and has excellent electrical insulating properties. It is used in oil-filled transformers (also called wet transformers).

Transformer Oil Functions

The functions of a transformer oil are: to insulate, to serve as a coolant (heat dissipation), and to suppress corona discharge and arcing.

Insulation

Transformer oils have a much higher dielectric strength than air. The insulating material is immersed in oil, which not only improves the insulation strength, but also protects it from moisture.

Heat dissipation

Transformer oils have a large specific heat and are often used as a coolant. The heat generated during the operation of the transformer causes the oil close to the iron core and windings to be heated and expanded. The heat is dissipated through a radiator to ensure the normal operation of the transformer.

Arc suppression

Arc suppression is the reduction of sparks formed when current-carrying contacts are separated. The spark is a luminous discharge of highly energized electrons and ions, and is an electric arc.

Typical Specifications for a Transformer Oil

  • Flash Point: 140° C or greater
  • Pour Point: -40° C or lower
  • Dielectric Breakdown Voltage: 30 kV (RMS) or greater

Flash Point

The flash point of a material is the lowest liquid temperature at which, under certain standardized conditions, a liquid gives off vapours in a quantity such as to be capable of forming an ignitable vapour/air mixture. Higher is better.

Pour Point

The pour point of a liquid is the temperature below which the liquid loses its flow characteristics. It is defined as the minimum temperature in which the oil has the ability to pour down from a beaker. Lower is better.

Dielectric Breakdown Voltage

It is the voltage at which dielectric breakdown occurs, meaning when the electrical field becomes high enough to cause the dielectric oil to abruptly switch from being an electrical insulator to a partial conductor. Higher is better.

It is also important that the transformer oil be non-corrosive towards copper, a widely used material in transformers.

A good transformer oil needs to meet all the requirements of the ANSI/ASTM D3487 Type II specification, and the IEC 62525 and ASTM D1275B anti-corrosion specifications.
